The third move is to build output channels that explain why the two snapshots differ. What happened between December 31, 2024, and December 31, 2025, that changed the entity's financial position?

The system answers this by decomposing the change in equities — the residual — into categories. Equities can change for several reasons, and each reason tells a different part of the story.

Operations

The entity earned revenues and incurred expenses. Revenues are the gross inflows of worth resulting from the entity's activities — selling goods, providing services, earning fees. Expenses are the gross outflows — the resources consumed in generating those revenues. If revenues exceeded expenses, operations increased equities. If expenses exceeded revenues, operations decreased equities.

The income statement reports this: what came in from operations, what went out, and what the difference was over the period. This is the channel that most people think of first when they ask "how did the entity do this year?" It is the system's attempt to show whether the entity created or consumed worth through its activities.

Cash

Operations as reported on the income statement do not necessarily move in lockstep with cash. A company can record a sale (revenue) before it collects the cash — the customer owes the money but has not paid yet. It can incur an expense (like depreciation) that involves no cash movement at all. Revenue and expense are conventions for recognizing worth created and consumed. Cash is a fact about what actually moved.

The statement of cash flows tracks the actual cash that moved in and out during the period. It typically groups these movements into three categories: cash from operating activities, cash used for investing (buying or selling long-term assets), and cash from financing (borrowing, repaying debt, transactions with owners). Under the indirect method — the most common approach — it begins with the income statement's bottom line and adjusts for everything that affected reported income but not cash, and everything that affected cash but not reported income. It is, in effect, a reconciliation: here is what the income statement said happened, and here is what actually happened in cash.

Owner transactions and other changes

Not all changes in equities come from operations or cash movements. Owners may invest additional capital (increasing equities) or receive distributions — dividends — (decreasing equities). There may be gains or losses that bypass the income statement under certain conventions. The statement of changes in equities tracks all of this: it starts with opening equities, accounts for every category of change, and arrives at closing equities. It is the full bridge from one balance sheet to the next. (In a not-for-profit, a statement of activities and changes in net assets serves a comparable role — the architecture is the same, as we saw in Section 1.)
